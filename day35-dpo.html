🎯 DPO — align straight from preferences: one classification-style loss, no reward model, no RL loop

Yesterday alignment took three models and an RL loop. DPO does the same job with one loss.

RLHF (Day 34) aligns a model in three stages: fine-tune, train a separate reward model on preference pairs, then run PPO to chase that reward on a KL leash. DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) proved you don't need the middle two: there's a mathematical shortcut that lets you optimize the same preference pairs straight into the policy with a single classification-style loss. No reward model, no reinforcement learning — just push up the log-probability of the chosen answer over the rejected one, measured against a frozen reference. Train it below and watch the loss fall while the chosen−rejected margin grows. Every gradient step is real code running in your browser.

🔨 Build DPO — the whole thing is one loss function

    
DPO takes the same inputs as RLHF — an SFT model and a dataset of (chosen, rejected) preference pairs — and deletes the two hardest parts: the separate reward model and the PPO loop. What's left is a single classification-style loss you minimize with ordinary gradient descent. Here it is in annotated pseudo-code.

    
    
      
  1. 
        
    1
    Same ingredients as RLHF — minus the reward model
    
        
    Start from the SFT model and the preference dataset. Two copies of the SFT weights: one is the trainable policy, the other is frozen as the reference. That frozen reference is the only "second model" DPO ever needs — and it never trains.
    
        
    sft   = load("sft-model")                       # instruction-tuned base (Day 34, stage 1)
prefs = [(prompt, chosen, rejected), ...]        # the SAME preference pairs RLHF collects

policy = copy(sft)                               # the model we will train
ref    = freeze(copy(sft))                       # frozen reference — baked into the loss
# NOTE: no reward model is ever built. That's the whole point.
    
      
    2. 
      
  2. 
        
    2
    The implicit reward — reward defined by the policy itself
    
        
    DPO's key trick: instead of training a reward model, define the reward in terms of how much more likely the policy makes an answer than the reference does. This is exactly the reward the optimal RLHF policy would have implied — you get it for free, no training.
    
        
    # implicit reward of an answer y for prompt x:
#   how much the policy raised its log-prob above the reference, scaled by beta
def implicit_reward(x, y):
    return beta * (policy.logprob(y|x) - ref.logprob(y|x))
# high when the policy loves y MORE than the reference did.
# beta scales it — and, as we'll see, controls the KL leash for free.
    
      
    3. 
      
  3. 
        
    3
    The DPO loss — Bradley–Terry, straight on the policy
    
        
    RLHF trained a reward model with the Bradley–Terry loss −log σ(r_chosen − r_rejected). DPO plugs the implicit reward into that very same loss. Because the implicit reward is the policy's own log-prob gap to the reference, minimizing this loss trains the policy directly. It's a binary classification loss: "is chosen preferred to rejected?"
    
        
    def dpo_loss(x, y_chosen, y_rejected):
    # log-prob margins vs the frozen reference (this IS the implicit reward / beta)
    d_chosen   = policy.logprob(y_chosen  |x) - ref.logprob(y_chosen  |x)
    d_rejected = policy.logprob(y_rejected|x) - ref.logprob(y_rejected|x)
    margin = beta * (d_chosen - d_rejected)      # r(chosen) - r(rejected)
    return -log_sigmoid(margin)                  # push chosen above rejected
# identical shape to the reward-model loss — but it trains the POLICY.
    
      
    4. 
      
  4. 
        
    4
    β — the KL leash, folded into the loss
    
        
    In RLHF the KL penalty was a separate term you subtracted from the reward during PPO. In DPO the same regularization is already inside the loss via β, which scales the log-prob gap into the reward margin m = β·Δ. Large β keeps the policy glued to the reference: a small gap already saturates the loss, so it barely moves. Small β lets the log-prob gap blow up — only a large gap satisfies the loss, so the policy strays far and prefers chosen answers hard, at the risk of degenerating.
    
        
    # beta plays the SAME role as RLHF's KL coefficient — but for free.
# it scales the log-prob gap (delta) into the reward margin: m = beta * delta
# large beta -> a SMALL delta already makes a big margin -> policy stays near ref
# small beta -> only a LARGE delta satisfies the loss  -> gap grows HUGE, big drift
#
# there is no explicit KL term and no reference sampling during a step.
# the reference log-probs are computed once per batch; the leash is implicit.
    
      
    5. 
      
  5. 
        
    5
    The training loop — plain supervised learning
    
        
    No rollouts, no sampling from the policy, no advantage estimation, no clipped RL update. Just iterate over the preference pairs in mini-batches and take a normal gradient step, exactly like training a classifier. Stable, cheap, and reproducible.
    
        
    for batch in dataloader(prefs):                  # ordinary supervised mini-batches
    loss = mean(dpo_loss(x, yc, yr) for (x, yc, yr) in batch)
    loss.backward()                              # one backward pass
    optimizer.step()                             # one gradient step — that's it
    optimizer.zero_grad()
return policy                                    # the aligned model
# contrast Day 34: no policy.generate(), no reward_model(), no ppo_update().
    
      
    6. 
      
  6. 
        
    6
    What DPO removed — and what to watch for
    
        
    DPO deletes the reward model and the PPO loop, which removes reward-hacking of a separate proxy and most of RLHF's instability. It isn't free of pitfalls: with small β the chosen/rejected log-probs can both fall (it drives them apart, not necessarily up), so quality can degrade if you over-train. Pick β well and it matches or beats PPO-RLHF at a fraction of the cost — which is why it's the default for most open models.
    
        
    # DPO removes, vs RLHF (Day 34):
#   - the separate reward model      (no proxy to hack)
#   - the PPO reinforcement loop      (no sampling, no clipping, far more stable)
#   - the online reward-vs-KL balance (the KL leash is now implicit in beta)
# still tune beta + watch for over-training (chosen & rejected both sinking).
# cousins that push further: IPO, KTO, ORPO, and RLAIF/Constitutional AI.

🎉 Day 35 of AIFromZero done.
You saw how DPO collapses RLHF's three-stage pipeline into a single loss. The trick is the implicit reward: β·(log πθ − log πref) — reward defined by the policy's own log-prob gap to a frozen reference, no reward model needed. Drop that into the Bradley–Terry preference loss and minimizing it trains the policy directly to raise the log-prob margin of the chosen answer over the rejected one. β is the KL leash, now folded straight into the loss: large β hugs the reference, small β lets it stray far. No sampling, no PPO, no proxy reward to hack — just gradient descent, which is why DPO (and its cousins) align most open models today. 👉 Tomorrow — Day 36: Constitutional AI & RLAIF — what if the labeller isn't a human at all?
