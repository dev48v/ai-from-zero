🎓 Knowledge Distillation — Teach a Small Model to Mimic a Big One

Watch a tiny "student" learn from a big "teacher" — not just the answer, but the doubt.

A big teacher model looks at an image labelled dog and outputs a whole probability distribution: dog, but also a bit of wolf and a sliver of cat. Those wrong-class probabilities are the "dark knowledge" — the teacher is telling the student that a dog is more like a wolf than a cat. A temperature knob softens that distribution so the student can see it. Then we train a much smaller student to match it. Everything below is real: real softmax, real temperature, real gradient descent.

1 · The teacher's soft labels

True label: 🐕 dog. The teacher's raw scores (logits) run through softmax(logit / T). Drag temperature and watch the distribution soften.

Temperature T 1.0

T = 1 is the normal output (spiky, near one-hot). Higher T flattens the distribution so the small differences between wolf and cat become visible — that's the extra signal we hand the student. Entropy: — bits.

The student is a small softmax classifier over a handful of 2-D points across 3 classes. The teacher has already "seen" everything; the student must learn a decision boundary that agrees with the teacher's soft targets.

The loss curve is the student's real cross-entropy against the teacher's soft targets, computed every epoch. Train on soft targets and the student not only gets the labels right but also lines up its whole distribution with the teacher's (high "agrees" score) — it inherited the dark knowledge. Train on hard labels only and it can still get the answer right, but it never learns the teacher's nuance. Same tiny model, more knowledge transferred, for free.

🔨 Distill a model yourself

    
The recipe is short: get a good teacher, capture its soft outputs, and train a small student to match them. Here's the whole loop.

    
    
      
  1. 
        
    1
    Pick a strong teacher & a small student
    
        
    The teacher is any model that's already good (large, slow, expensive). The student is a smaller architecture — fewer layers, narrower, or fewer parameters — that you actually want to ship. DistilBERT, for instance, is a 6-layer student of 12-layer BERT.
    
        
    teacher = load("bert-large")        # accurate but heavy
student = SmallTransformer(layers=6) # what we want to serve
    
      
    2. 
      
  2. 
        
    2
    Capture the teacher's SOFT targets
    
        
    Run the teacher over your data and save the full probability distribution — divided by a temperature T>1 so the wrong-class probabilities (the dark knowledge) are visible. These, not the raw labels, are what the student learns from.
    
        
    T = 4.0
with torch.no_grad():
    logits_t = teacher(x)
    soft_targets = softmax(logits_t / T, dim=-1)  # keep the whole vector
    
      
    3. 
      
  3. 
        
    3
    The distillation loss = soft + hard
    
        
    The student minimises KL divergence to the teacher's softened distribution (scaled by T² so gradients stay balanced), usually blended with a little ordinary cross-entropy on the true label. α mixes the two.
    
        
    p_s = softmax(student(x) / T, dim=-1)
soft_loss = KL(soft_targets, p_s) * (T * T)   # match the teacher
hard_loss = cross_entropy(student(x), y)      # stay grounded in truth
loss = alpha * soft_loss + (1 - alpha) * hard_loss
    
      
    4. 
      
  4. 
        
    4
    Train the student, then ship it alone
    
        
    Backprop only through the student — the teacher is frozen. After training you throw the teacher away and deploy the small student, which now runs several times faster and cheaper while keeping most of the teacher's accuracy.
    
        
    for x, y in loader:
    loss = distill_loss(student, teacher, x, y, T=4, alpha=0.9)
    loss.backward(); optimizer.step()   # teacher stays frozen
# deploy student only → smaller, faster, cheaper
    
      
    5. 
      
  5. 
        
    5
    Know the limits
    
        
    A student can't exceed the teacher, so distillation inherits the teacher's mistakes and biases. There's a floor on how small you can go before accuracy craters, and you need enough (unlabelled is fine) data to expose the teacher's behaviour. It's a compression tool, not a way to create new capability.
    
        
    # rules of thumb
#   student accuracy ≤ teacher accuracy
#   too small a student → quality collapses
#   unlabelled data still works (teacher supplies targets)
#   biases transfer too — audit the student
